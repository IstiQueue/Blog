Failosophy Book Review

Failures are usually identified with negativities, but what if I tell you that it could be the opposite? This is my review of the book "Failosophy".

What’s one thing people want to feel the least but realistically everybody gets to feel it in any type of form? Failure. There is no way you can avoid failure, one has to feel it at least once in their life. Failures are usually identified with negativities, but what if I tell you that it could be the opposite? What if there is an alternate way of seeing it? What if I told you there is a next chapter after the chapter of failure? Let me introduce you to failosophy, a book written by Elizabeth Day, the bestselling author of ‘How To Fail’.

So what is failosophy exactly? It is a big pile compilation of different principles, perspectives, and advice Elizabeth Day has found as a podcaster, or a journalist in general. In which she hoped to be inspirational for the reader and hopefully guide them to be more open with their failures. But of course, it also contains stories about the author as well. What makes it ironic is that, usually when an influential person writes a book, they will write about how they got into the position they are in right now, Elizabeth Day wrote about the lowest point in their life.

As humans, we often stumble upon unexpected obstacles, and failure is one of them. It is common and completely humane for us to be affected by it, but what makes us extraordinary is if we turn them around, and see it in a different light. A light to see that it can be a factor for you to develop and improve for the future. This book encourages you to do so, as I quote directly from the book, “Once you admit your own vulnerabilities and choose to share them, they are no longer fearful things to avoid: the adult equivalent of the bogeyman under the bed.”

Vulnerabilities are usually overlooked as weak, when in reality it functions as a bridge between a great weakness and strength of ourselves. Seeking the courage to walk and own the bridge is hard, but once you admit and embrace it, each step you take in the bridge will feel as light as a feather. The talk of this topic is one out of seven principles Elizabeth Day come up with the full list;

  1. Failure is just is
  2. You are not your worst thoughts
  3. Almost everyone feels they've failed at their twenties
  4. Break ups are not a tragedy
  5. Failure is data acquisition
  6. There is no such thing as a future you
  7. Being open about our vulnerabilities is the source of true strength

The more I turned the page, the more I realized how relatable the entire book is to my life. The way each principle could be applied to each problem of my life proved how I am more than certain to recommend the book to the fellow youngsters out there. All in hope to be not attached with the haunted feeling of failure, befriend it, or even dethrone it. (sff)

Saffana Nur Aulia
